Caregiver Resources

Support, financial assistance, legal tools, and tech that actually helps — everything a family caregiver needs in one place.

By TechForDad · Updated June 2026

Caregiver burnout crept up on me before I saw it coming. The hardest part is that nobody warns you in advance. These are the resources I found after I was already exhausted — sharing them now so you can find them earlier than I did.

You're Not Alone — Even When It Feels That Way

Over 53 million Americans provide unpaid care to an adult family member. Most do it without adequate support, financial assistance, or even the knowledge that help exists. This guide pulls together the resources that matter most — the ones that have made a real difference to caregivers we've spoken with.

Financial Assistance for Caregivers

Medicaid Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS)

If your parent qualifies for Medicaid, many states offer Home and Community-Based Services waivers that fund in-home care, adult day programs, home modifications, and in some states, payment to family caregivers. Eligibility and covered services vary significantly by state. Contact your State Medicaid office or use benefits.gov to check eligibility.

VA Aid and Attendance Benefit

Veterans who need help with daily activities (bathing, dressing, eating) may qualify for the VA Aid & Attendance benefit — a monthly pension supplement that can reach $2,400/month for a veteran with a dependent. This is significantly underutilized. If your parent is a veteran, apply at va.gov or contact a Veterans Service Organization (VSO) for free assistance.

Area Agency on Aging (AAA)

Every county in the US has an Area Agency on Aging funded by the Older Americans Act. They coordinate local services including meal programs, transportation, home repair assistance, and caregiver support. Find your local AAA at eldercare.acl.gov or by calling the Eldercare Locator: 1-800-677-1116.

PACE (Program of All-Inclusive Care for the Elderly)

PACE provides comprehensive medical and social services to seniors who are eligible for nursing home-level care but wish to remain in the community. It can coordinate all medical care, physical therapy, social activities, and transportation — significantly reducing caregiver burden. Availability varies by location.

Respite Care — Taking a Break Without Guilt

Caregiver burnout is real and it's dangerous — both to your health and to the quality of care your parent receives. Respite care provides temporary relief for caregivers.

  • In-home respite — a paid or volunteer caregiver comes to your parent's home for a few hours or days. The ARCH National Respite Network (archrespite.org) can locate programs in your area.
  • Adult day centers — your parent attends a structured program during daytime hours. Typically $70–100/day; some Medicaid programs cover this.
  • Short-term residential respite — some assisted living facilities and nursing homes offer short-term stays (1–4 weeks) for respite purposes.
  • National Respite Locator — archrespite.org/respite-locator to find options in your zip code.

Support Groups and Mental Health

  • Caregiver Action Network (caregiveraction.org) — resources, community forums, and a helpline (1-855-227-3640) specifically for family caregivers.
  • Alzheimer's Association (alz.org) — 24/7 helpline (1-800-272-3900), local support groups, and an online community for dementia caregivers specifically.
  • Well Spouse Association (wellspouse.org) — support for those caring for an ill or disabled spouse specifically.
  • Psychology Today therapist finder — filter for "caregiver" specialization and telehealth options. Caregiver-specific therapy is different from general counseling and worth seeking out.

Legal and Financial Planning

These are the documents every family should have in place before a health crisis — not after.

  • Durable Power of Attorney — authorizes a trusted person to make financial decisions if your parent becomes incapacitated. Without this, a court-supervised guardianship may be required.
  • Healthcare Proxy / Medical Power of Attorney — designates someone to make medical decisions if your parent cannot.
  • Living Will / Advance Directive — documents your parent's wishes for end-of-life care, including resuscitation, artificial nutrition, and mechanical ventilation preferences.
  • POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) — a medical order (signed by a physician) that translates advance directive wishes into actionable orders. More immediately enforceable than a living will in emergency situations.

An elder law attorney can draft all of these for $500–2,000 depending on complexity. The National Academy of Elder Law Attorneys (naela.org) has a directory. Many legal aid organizations offer these services free or low-cost for seniors who qualify.

Tech That Actually Helps Caregivers

Beyond the products we review for seniors, a few technologies specifically reduce caregiver burden:

  • Medical alert systems — reduce the anxiety of being away from your parent. Our top pick is Medical Guardian for at-home use. See our full review →
  • Medication management devices — automatic pill dispensers (like Hero or MedMinder) that dispense the correct medication at the right time and alert you if a dose is missed. These eliminate one of the most stressful daily caregiver responsibilities.
  • Remote monitoring systems — motion sensor systems that track your parent's daily activity patterns and alert you to unusual inactivity. See our Fall Prevention Tech guide.
  • Video calling devices — a dedicated video call device (Amazon Echo Show, Google Nest Hub) on a kitchen counter is more reliable than expecting a parent to navigate a phone for video calls.
  • GPS trackers — essential for any senior with dementia and wandering risk. See our GPS Tracker guide →

When It's Time to Consider More Support

Signs that the current caregiving arrangement may no longer be adequate:

  • Your parent has had two or more falls in the past six months
  • Medication management is becoming unreliable
  • Personal hygiene is being neglected
  • You are sleeping poorly, withdrawing from your own social life, or feeling resentment
  • Your parent is unsafe alone for more than a few hours

These are signals to explore additional in-home help, adult day programs, or to begin researching assisted living options — not signs of failure. Getting help earlier leads to better outcomes for both caregivers and seniors.
