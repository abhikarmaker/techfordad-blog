Hearing Aid Buying Guide for Seniors

What changed in 2022 makes this a very different purchase than it used to be — here's what you need to know before spending a dollar.

By TechForDad · Updated June 2026

ℹ️ Prices & product details change frequently. Always verify current pricing and availability directly with the retailer or provider before purchasing. See our affiliate disclosure.

My dad went from turning the TV up to full volume to having actual dinner conversations again. The right hearing aid made that happen — and since 2022, you no longer need a prescription to get one.

What Changed: OTC Hearing Aids Are Now Legal — No Prescription Needed

In 2022, the FDA created a new over-the-counter (OTC) hearing aid category, allowing adults with mild to moderate hearing loss to buy hearing aids directly — from Amazon, Best Buy, Walmart, or directly from manufacturers — without a prescription, audiologist visit, or professional fitting. This is genuinely significant. It has driven prices from $3,000–8,000 per pair (prescription) to $200–1,500 (OTC) for comparable mild-to-moderate performance.

This guide covers both categories honestly, so you can make the right choice for your parent's specific situation.

OTC Hearing Aids: Who They're Right For

OTC hearing aids work well for adults who:

  • Have mild to moderate hearing loss — difficulty hearing in noisy environments, asking people to repeat themselves, turning up the TV
  • Are comfortable with some self-setup — most OTC devices have a companion smartphone app for adjustment
  • Want to try hearing aids at low cost before committing to a prescription device
  • Have relatively symmetric hearing loss — similar loss in both ears

Best OTC options in 2026:

  • Jabra Enhance Plus ($799/pair) — excellent sound quality, iPhone and Android app, comfortable fit. Our top OTC pick for tech-comfortable seniors.
  • Sony CRE-10 ($999/pair) — strong brand reliability, simple app, good for those who trust the Sony name.
  • Lexie B2 (Powered by Bose) ($849/pair) — Bose audio expertise shows in sound quality. Strong self-fitting tools.
  • Eargo 7 ($1,650/pair) — technically still OTC but provides remote professional support. Good bridge between OTC and prescription.

Prescription Hearing Aids: When They're Worth the Cost

Prescription hearing aids — fitted by an audiologist — remain the right choice for:

  • Moderate to severe hearing loss — OTC devices are not approved for severe loss and won't perform adequately
  • Asymmetric hearing loss — significantly different loss in each ear requires custom programming
  • Seniors who've tried OTC and not gotten adequate benefit
  • Complex listening environments — frequent restaurant conversations, large gatherings, or music listening

If your parent needs prescription aids, the major brands to consider are Phonak, Oticon, ReSound, and Widex. Costco's Kirkland Signature hearing aids offer prescription-level quality at significantly lower cost (~$1,500/pair) — this is one of the best-kept secrets in the hearing aid world.

Does Medicare Cover Hearing Aids?

Original Medicare (Parts A and B) does not cover hearing aids. Medicare Advantage plans vary — some cover a portion or include a hearing aid benefit through a third-party network. Check your parent's specific plan. Some states have Medicaid hearing aid coverage for low-income seniors.

The Veterans Administration (VA) provides hearing aids free of charge to eligible veterans — if your parent is a veteran with hearing loss, this is worth pursuing before spending any money out-of-pocket.

Key Features to Look For

  • Rechargeable batteries — disposable hearing aid batteries are small and challenging for seniors with limited dexterity. Rechargeable devices simplify daily management significantly.
  • Bluetooth connectivity — streams audio directly from iPhone, Android, or TV. Meaningful for seniors who want to hear the television clearly without cranking the volume.
  • Trial period — reputable OTC brands offer 30–45 day returns. Do not buy from a source that doesn't offer a meaningful trial period.
  • App simplicity — if your parent will need to adjust settings themselves, the companion app matters. Test it before purchasing.
  • IP rating (water resistance) — an IP68 rating means the device can handle sweat, rain, and minor splashes.

Mistakes Most Families Make (and How to Avoid Them)

  • Buying prescription aids at a retail audiologist without comparing Costco — the markup at private audiologist offices can be substantial.
  • Skipping the trial period — hearing aid adjustment takes 2–4 weeks. Commit to wearing them consistently before judging whether they work.
  • Buying aids that are too complicated to manage — a technically superior device that your parent can't operate is less useful than a simpler one they'll actually wear.
  • Treating hearing aids as a one-time purchase — hearing loss typically progresses; aids bought for mild loss may not be adequate in 5 years.
