Home Safety Checklist for Seniors

A complete room-by-room walkthrough to find and fix the hazards most likely to cause falls and injuries at home.

By Abhijeit Karmaker · Updated June 2026

📋 Jump to: Bathroom · Kitchen · Bedroom · Stairs & Hallways · Living Room · Outdoors · Whole Home

Why This Matters

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death among adults over 65. The CDC reports that one in four seniors falls each year — and the vast majority happen at home, in rooms they've navigated thousands of times. The hazards are predictable. So are the fixes. Work through this checklist room by room. Fix what you can today for free; note what needs a purchase or a handyman.

🛁 Bathroom

The most dangerous room in any senior's home. Wet floors, narrow spaces, and the physical effort of getting in and out of the tub create a reliable fall environment.

  • ☐ Grab bars near the toilet — not a towel bar (those aren't load-bearing). Properly anchored grab bars rated to 250 lbs minimum.
  • ☐ Grab bars inside the shower or tub — one vertical bar at the entry point, one horizontal bar inside.
  • ☐ Non-slip bath mat inside and outside the tub — replace any mat that has lost grip on its underside.
  • ☐ Shower chair or tub bench — standing in a wet shower for 10 minutes is a significant fall risk. A $30–60 chair eliminates it.
  • ☐ Handheld showerhead — allows bathing while seated. Basic models cost under $30.
  • ☐ Raised toilet seat — adds 3–4 inches of height, reduces the effort required to sit and stand.
  • ☐ Motion-activated nightlight — bathroom trips at 2am are when falls happen. Automatic lighting removes the need to reach for a switch.
  • ☐ Medications stored safely — accessible to your parent without climbing, but secure from any visiting grandchildren.

🍳 Kitchen

Kitchen hazards center on reaching, bending, and the cognitive demands of cooking. Simplify the environment.

  • ☐ Frequently used items at counter height — plates, glasses, and daily-use items between hip and shoulder height. Nothing above the head or below the knee.
  • ☐ Step stool with a handle, or removed entirely — if a step stool is needed, replace it with one that has a grab rail. Better: rearrange so nothing requires climbing.
  • ☐ Non-slip mat at the sink — rubber backing; check it annually and replace when suction is lost.
  • ☐ Good task lighting over stove and counters — under-cabinet LED strips eliminate shadows where cooking accidents happen.
  • ☐ Stove knob covers or auto shut-off — if cognitive decline is a concern, covers prevent unintended activation and an automatic shut-off device adds a second layer of protection.
  • ☐ Working smoke and CO detectors — test monthly. Replace batteries annually or switch to 10-year sealed units.

🛏️ Bedroom

Most bedroom falls happen at night — getting up for the bathroom in a disoriented state.

  • ☐ Motion-activated nightlights on the path to the bathroom — plug-in motion-sensor lights in the bedroom and hallway; no reaching for switches in the dark.
  • ☐ Medical alert device or phone within arm's reach of the bed — reachable from the floor in case of a fall. A bedside medical alert button is the safest option.
  • ☐ Bed height appropriate — feet should touch the floor when sitting on the edge. Use bed risers if too low; lower the frame if too high.
  • ☐ No loose rugs between bed and bathroom — remove or fully secure with non-slip tape on all four sides.
  • ☐ Easy-to-operate lamp — large base switch or touch-activated lamp rather than a small knob.
  • ☐ Cords off the floor — secure charging cables and lamp cords along baseboards.
  • ☐ Chair or bench for dressing — dressing while seated is safer than balancing on one foot.

🪜 Stairs & Hallways

Stairs cause a disproportionate share of serious fall injuries. If your parent's home has stairs, this section is critical.

  • ☐ Handrails on both sides of every staircase — many homes only have one. Adding a second is a standard carpentry job.
  • ☐ Handrails extend the full length of stairs — rails that stop before the bottom step cause falls at that last step.
  • ☐ Bright lighting at the top and bottom — both switches accessible without going up or down first.
  • ☐ No loose carpet on stairs — any lifting, bubbling, or fraying should be repaired or replaced immediately.
  • ☐ Non-slip strips on each step — especially on hardwood or tile stairs.
  • ☐ Nothing stored on stairs — a single item left on a step is a reliable trip hazard.
  • ☐ Clear hallway paths — walkers and canes need 36 inches minimum of clear passage.

🛋️ Living Room

  • ☐ No loose rugs or curling rug corners — remove area rugs or secure every edge with non-slip tape and a rug pad underneath.
  • ☐ Clear walking paths between key furniture — unobstructed path from the main chair to the door, bathroom, and kitchen.
  • ☐ Chair height allows easy sitting and standing — firm cushions or chair risers can correct a seat that's too low.
  • ☐ Phone reachable from the main sitting area — and from the floor in case of a fall.
  • ☐ All cords secured along baseboards — not crossing any walking paths.

🏡 Outdoor & Entry

  • ☐ Front steps in good repair — patch cracked or uneven concrete; add non-slip strips to smooth surfaces.
  • ☐ Handrail on all entry steps — even a single step benefits from a rail.
  • ☐ Motion-activated outdoor lighting — well-lit entry paths prevent falls arriving home after dark.
  • ☐ No tripping hazards on pathways — cracked concrete, tree roots, and uneven pavers should be repaired or clearly marked.
  • ☐ Doormat secured flat — rubber-backed, checked regularly for bunching.
  • ☐ Keyless entry or large-button keypad — fumbling with keys at the door is a fall risk. A keypad lock eliminates it.

🏠 Whole Home

  • ☐ Smoke detectors on every level — tested monthly, batteries replaced annually.
  • ☐ Carbon monoxide detector near sleeping areas — required by law in many states; essential everywhere.
  • ☐ Emergency contact list posted visibly — on the fridge or near the main phone. Include family, doctor, pharmacy, and poison control.
  • ☐ Medical alert system active and worn — the most important safety net for any senior who is home alone regularly.

What to Fix First

Prioritize in this order: (1) Free fixes today — clear a path, remove a rug, move items to a reachable shelf. (2) Low-cost purchases — nightlights, non-slip mats, grab bars ($15–80 each). (3) Handyman work — installing handrails, reinforcing stair carpet, anchoring grab bars properly into wall studs.

If you're overwhelmed, start with the bathroom and stairs. Those two areas account for the majority of serious senior falls.
