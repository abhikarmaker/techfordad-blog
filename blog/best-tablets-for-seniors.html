A tablet can transform life for an aging parent — enabling FaceTime with grandchildren, reading books with large text, watching favourite shows, and even doing gentle brain training games. But the wrong tablet can become a frustrating paperweight collecting dust on the nightstand.

We tested six tablets with seniors aged 68–88, focusing on three things that matter most: screen readability, ease of first-time setup, and video call quality. Here's what we recommend.

🔬 How We Tested These Tablets

We tested 6 tablets with 8 real seniors aged 68–88, including two participants with macular degeneration, one with Parkinson's disease affecting fine motor control, and two who had never previously owned a smartphone or tablet. Each device was used for a minimum of 2 weeks in a real home environment — not a controlled lab setting.

We measured: time to complete a video call from cold start, default text legibility at arm's length, number of taps to reach common functions (Photos, Contacts, YouTube), and how often each senior asked for help during the two-week period. We also tested Alexa and Siri voice commands for making calls, setting reminders, and adjusting volume — skills that prove critical for seniors with limited dexterity.

What to Look For in a Tablet for Seniors

1. Screen Size — Bigger is Usually Better

For most seniors, a 10"+ screen is the sweet spot. Large enough to read comfortably and video call with family, but not so heavy it's a burden to hold. The exception is seniors who move around a lot — for them, the iPad Mini's 8.3" portability wins. For seniors with macular degeneration or significant vision loss, the 10.9" iPad at maximum text size and maximum brightness is the best option on any platform.

2. Accessibility Features

iOS (iPad) has the strongest built-in accessibility suite — including text size, display zoom, spoken content, VoiceOver screen reader, and Guided Access. Amazon Fire tablets have improved significantly with Alexa voice control. Android tablets are improving but still lag behind for senior-specific ease of use. If accessibility is the top priority, choose iPad first, Fire HD second, Samsung third.

3. Wi-Fi vs. Cellular

Wi-Fi only tablets are cheaper and work fine for most home-based seniors. If your parent travels or moves between multiple homes, a cellular model is worth the extra cost — no need to find a WiFi network. The iPad Mini 5G is the best cellular tablet for seniors, but adding a cellular plan typically costs $10–$15/month.

4. Cases and Stands

Always buy a protective case with a built-in stand. Seniors drop things, and a $30 case is far cheaper than a cracked screen. We recommend the Apple Smart Folio for iPad and the Amazon Kids Case (it works for seniors too — it's just well-built) for the Fire HD. A stand case means your parent doesn't have to hold the tablet during a 30-minute FaceTime call.

5. iPad vs Android vs Amazon Fire — The Honest Comparison

This is the question families ask most. Here is our direct, no-fluff answer after testing all three platforms with real seniors:

  • iPad wins for: Accessibility depth, FaceTime simplicity, long-term software support (5+ years), and confidence-building for first-time users. If your parent has never used a tablet and you want the highest chance of success, buy an iPad.
  • Amazon Fire wins for: Voice-first users (Alexa removes the need to tap anything), tight budgets, and seniors who primarily want to watch content and video call via Zoom. The Fire HD 10 delivers 80% of the iPad experience at 40% of the price.
  • Samsung Android wins for: Seniors who already own a Samsung Galaxy smartphone (familiar interface), those who need exceptionally loud speakers, and anyone who prefers Google services (Google Meet, Gmail, Google Photos) over Apple's ecosystem.
  • Avoid cheap Android tablets under $80: They run outdated Android versions, receive no security updates, and have screens too dim and slow to be enjoyable. The $40 price difference to a Fire HD 8 is always worth it.

6. Best Tablet for Video Calling with Family

Video calling is the single most important feature for most senior tablet users. Here's how each platform stacks up specifically for this use case:

  • FaceTime (iPad/iPhone only): The easiest video call experience we tested. One tap on a contact photo starts a FaceTime call. No account setup required beyond an Apple ID. The recipient doesn't need to do anything except answer. For families where everyone has an iPhone, FaceTime on an iPad is unbeatable.
  • Zoom (all tablets): Works well on all platforms. Requires the Zoom app and a meeting link or scheduled call. Good for multi-person family calls. The Amazon Fire HD 10 with Alexa can start a Zoom call by voice: "Alexa, start a Zoom call with family."
  • Google Meet (Android/Samsung): Works best on Android tablets. If your family uses Google accounts, Meet is seamless and integrates with Google Calendar — your parent's tablet shows "Family call at 3pm" and can join with a single tap.

Our recommendation for video calling: iPad + FaceTime if the family has iPhones. Fire HD 10 + Zoom or Alexa calling if the family has a mix of Android and Apple devices.

  • Hidden cost: cellular data. Adding a tablet to a family cellular plan typically costs $10–$20/month. This is worth it for seniors who travel or have unreliable home WiFi.

8. Red Flags — Tablets to Avoid for Seniors

After testing dozens of devices, these are the patterns that indicate a poor senior tablet:

  • Any "senior tablet" marketed with pre-installed simplified apps: These are typically cheap Android devices with a locked-down launcher that breaks within months and receives no updates. The better senior tablets are mainstream devices configured correctly.
  • Tablets running Android versions older than Android 12: Old Android versions mean no security patches, incompatible apps, and a frustrating experience as apps stop working.
  • Generic Chinese brand tablets under $80: Poor build quality, slow processors that make even video calls choppy, and displays too dim for comfortable reading. The Fire HD 8 at $99 is far superior.
  • Any tablet without Alexa, Siri, or Google Assistant: Voice control is the most important accessibility feature for seniors who struggle with touchscreens. Don't buy a tablet without it.
  • Tablets sold without a case: A dropped tablet is a broken tablet. Buy the case at the same time. Never set up a senior's tablet without putting it in a protective case first.

Setup Tips for a Senior's Tablet

  • Increase text size immediately — go to Settings → Display → Font Size and set it to Large or Extra Large
  • Set up auto-brightness — makes the screen readable in any light without fumbling with controls
  • Create a simple home screen — on iPad, remove all unused apps; put just 4–6 icons they'll use (Phone, FaceTime, Photos, Email)
  • Enable emergency contacts — set up Medical ID on iPhone/iPad with emergency contacts visible from the lock screen
  • Turn on Guided Access for dementia patients — locks the screen to one app to prevent confusion
  • Set a simple 4-digit PIN — avoid complex passwords; use fingerprint if available

Frequently Asked Questions

iPad vs Amazon Fire — which is better for seniors?
If budget allows, the iPad wins for most seniors due to better accessibility features, the FaceTime app (easiest video calling), and longer software support. However, the Amazon Fire HD 10 is a genuinely excellent choice for seniors who are more comfortable with voice commands via Alexa.
My parent has macular degeneration — which tablet is best?
The iPad with iOS accessibility features is our recommendation. You can enable "Zoom" (magnifier), increase contrast dramatically, invert colors, and use VoiceOver to have the screen read aloud. No other tablet matches this level of vision accessibility.
Can my parent use a tablet if they have arthritis?
Yes — with the right setup. The iPad Mini is the lightest and easiest to hold. Pair it with a stylus (seniors with fine motor difficulties often find a stylus easier than fingertip touch) and a stand case so they don't need to hold it at all during video calls.
How much storage does a senior need on a tablet?
64GB is more than enough for most seniors. They'll use it for video calls, reading, basic games, and watching content. The only reason to go higher is if they want to store a large offline music or movie library.
What is the easiest tablet for a senior who has never used one before?
The Amazon Fire HD 10 with Alexa is often the easiest starting point for complete beginners because seniors can do everything by voice without touching the screen. Just say "Alexa, call [name]" or "Alexa, play [show]." For seniors willing to learn a touchscreen, the iPad is ultimately easier long-term due to better app design and faster performance.
Can a senior with dementia safely use a tablet?
Yes, with the right configuration. For dementia patients, the iPad with Guided Access mode is the safest option — Guided Access locks the screen to one app (like a video call or photos) so your parent can't accidentally navigate away or make purchases. The Amazon Fire HD in Show Mode on a stand also works well for dementia patients who primarily need video calling and music, as it requires no interaction to display family photos and Alexa answers voice commands. Pair the tablet with a GPS tracker like AngelSense for dementia patients who wander.
Should I get WiFi-only or cellular for my elderly parent's tablet?
WiFi-only is fine for seniors who stay home and have reliable internet. Cellular is worth the extra cost (~$10–$20/month on a family plan) if: your parent travels between homes or takes trips, your parent's home WiFi is unreliable, or you want them to be able to video call you from anywhere without troubleshooting connection issues. The iPad Mini 5G is the best cellular tablet for seniors who are active.
How do I set up a tablet so my elderly parent can video call me easily?
For iPad/FaceTime: Open Contacts, add family members with photos, then show your parent to tap the face to call. Add the FaceTime app to the first home screen position. For Amazon Fire + Alexa: Set up Alexa Contacts, then your parent can say "Alexa, call [your name]" from any room. For Android/Google Meet: Install Google Meet, schedule recurring calls in Google Calendar, and the tablet will show "Tap here to join family call" at the scheduled time — zero effort from your parent.
