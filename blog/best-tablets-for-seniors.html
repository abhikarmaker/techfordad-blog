A tablet can transform life for an aging parent — enabling FaceTime with grandchildren, reading books with large text, watching favourite shows, and even doing gentle brain training games. But the wrong tablet can become a frustrating paperweight collecting dust on the nightstand.

We tested six tablets with seniors aged 68–88, focusing on three things that matter most: screen readability, ease of first-time setup, and video call quality. Here's what we recommend.

The iPad wins for seniors for one main reason: iOS accessibility. Within the Settings app, you can increase text size, enable high contrast, turn on spoken descriptions, and enable a simplified "Guided Access" mode that locks the tablet to one app — so a parent with dementia stays on their video call without accidentally navigating away.

The 10.9" Retina display is also the most readable screen we tested, with excellent brightness even in sunlit rooms. If budget allows, the iPad is the right long-term investment — it'll receive software updates for at least 5 years.

What to Look For in a Tablet for Seniors

Screen Size — Bigger is Usually Better

For most seniors, a 10"+ screen is the sweet spot. Large enough to read comfortably and video call with family, but not so heavy it's a burden to hold. The exception is seniors who move around a lot — for them, the iPad Mini's 8.3" portability wins.

Accessibility Features

iOS (iPad) has the strongest built-in accessibility suite — including text size, display zoom, spoken content, and Guided Access. Amazon Fire tablets have improved significantly with Alexa voice control. Android tablets are improving but still lag behind for senior-specific ease of use.

Wi-Fi vs. Cellular

Wi-Fi only tablets are cheaper and work fine for most home-based seniors. If your parent travels or moves between multiple homes, a cellular model is worth the extra cost — no need to find a WiFi network.

Cases and Stands

Always buy a protective case with a built-in stand. Seniors drop things, and a $30 case is far cheaper than a cracked screen. We recommend the Apple Smart Folio for iPad and the Amazon Kids Case (it works for seniors too — it's just well-built) for the Fire HD.

Setup Tips for a Senior's Tablet

  • Increase text size immediately — go to Settings → Display → Font Size and set it to Large or Extra Large
  • Set up auto-brightness — makes the screen readable in any light without fumbling with controls
  • Create a simple home screen — on iPad, remove all unused apps; put just 4–6 icons they'll use (Phone, FaceTime, Photos, Email)
  • Enable emergency contacts — set up Medical ID on iPhone/iPad with emergency contacts visible from the lock screen
  • Turn on Guided Access for dementia patients — locks the screen to one app to prevent confusion
  • Set a simple 4-digit PIN — avoid complex passwords; use fingerprint if available

Frequently Asked Questions

iPad vs Amazon Fire — which is better for seniors?
If budget allows, the iPad wins for most seniors due to better accessibility features, the FaceTime app (easiest video calling), and longer software support. However, the Amazon Fire HD 10 is a genuinely excellent choice for seniors who are more comfortable with voice commands via Alexa.
My parent has macular degeneration — which tablet is best?
The iPad with iOS accessibility features is our recommendation. You can enable "Zoom" (magnifier), increase contrast dramatically, invert colors, and use VoiceOver to have the screen read aloud. No other tablet matches this level of vision accessibility.
Can my parent use a tablet if they have arthritis?
Yes — with the right setup. The iPad Mini is the lightest and easiest to hold. Pair it with a stylus (seniors with fine motor difficulties often find a stylus easier than fingertip touch) and a stand case so they don't need to hold it at all during video calls.
How much storage does a senior need on a tablet?
64GB is more than enough for most seniors. They'll use it for video calls, reading, basic games, and watching content. The only reason to go higher is if they want to store a large offline music or movie library.
