When my father-in-law mentioned he was turning the TV up to volume 45, we knew something had to change. Navigating hearing aids in Canada — provincial subsidies, audiologists vs. Costco, OTC options — took us weeks. I wrote this guide so you don't have to start from scratch.

Hearing loss is one of the most common — and most commonly ignored — health concerns facing Canadian seniors. According to Statistics Canada, roughly half of adults over 60 have some degree of hearing loss, yet fewer than one in five who could benefit from hearing aids actually uses them. The barrier is often cost: a quality pair of prescription hearing aids in Canada typically runs between $2,500 and $6,000 CAD.

The good news: Canada has meaningful provincial subsidy programs, Costco Canada offers excellent value through its Hearing Centres, and as of 2026, British Columbia became the first province to allow over-the-counter (OTC) hearing aids — a shift that is expected to spread to other provinces within the next year or two. This guide covers the best hearing aids available in Canada right now, with Canadian prices and the provincial funding information you need to make an informed decision.

Over-the-Counter Hearing Aids in Canada: 2026 Update

This is an area changing fast. Here's what you need to know:

British Columbia leads the way

In June 2026, BC became the first province in Canada to allow the sale of OTC hearing aids to adults 18 and older with mild to moderate hearing loss — without requiring a prescription or visit to an audiologist. The devices are Health Canada-approved and can be purchased at select retailers and online.

Ontario is next

Ontario launched a 30-day public consultation in April 2026 on whether to allow OTC hearing aid sales. A regulatory change in Ontario is expected in late 2026 or 2027. Other provinces are watching BC's rollout closely.

Apple AirPods Pro 2 — Not Yet Available in Canada as OTC Hearing Aids

Health Canada approved the hearing aid feature built into AirPods Pro 2 earbuds in late 2024, but as of mid-2026, Apple has not activated this feature for Canadian users. This is unlike the US, where the AirPods Pro 2 hearing aid mode has been available since late 2024. Keep an eye on Apple Canada's announcements — when this feature launches here, it will represent a genuinely accessible entry point for mild hearing loss at around $349 CAD.

Our take on OTC for Canadian seniors

OTC hearing aids are best suited for adults with mild to moderate hearing loss who are tech-comfortable enough to use a smartphone app for self-fitting. If your parent has more significant hearing loss, or if they find technology frustrating, a prescription hearing aid fitted by an audiologist will almost always deliver a better outcome. The provincial subsidy programs (Ontario ADP, Alberta AADL, Manitoba) apply to prescription aids — many OTC devices do not yet qualify.

Canadian Hearing Aid Buying Guide

Start with a proper hearing test

Before buying anything, your parent needs an audiogram — a professional hearing test that maps their specific hearing loss across different frequencies. In Canada, audiology assessments are often covered under provincial health plans when referred by a family doctor (check your province). The audiogram tells you the degree and type of hearing loss, which determines which aids are appropriate. Don't skip this step.

Understanding hearing aid types

  • RIC / RITE (Receiver-in-Canal / Receiver-in-the-Ear) — The most popular style for seniors. A small unit sits behind the ear with a thin wire leading to a tiny speaker in the ear canal. Comfortable, discreet, and compatible with Bluetooth. Most of the products in this guide are RIC style.
  • BTE (Behind-the-Ear) — Larger unit, more powerful, often recommended for severe to profound hearing loss. More visible but easier to handle for seniors with dexterity issues.
  • ITE / ITC / CIC (In-the-Ear styles) — Custom-molded to fit entirely in the ear. More discreet but harder to handle, and some models don't offer Bluetooth.

Rechargeable vs. disposable batteries

We almost always recommend rechargeable hearing aids for Canadian seniors. Disposable batteries (size 312 or 10) are tiny and difficult to handle, especially with arthritis. Rechargeable aids sit in a charging case overnight — simpler, safer, and no battery runs out in the middle of the day. Most 2026 models offer rechargeable options.

Bluetooth matters — but check compatibility

Bluetooth streaming lets hearing aids receive audio directly from a smartphone or TV. Most modern hearing aids stream directly from iPhones; Android compatibility varies by brand. If your parent has an Android phone, confirm Bluetooth compatibility before purchasing. Phonak and Jabra have the broadest Android compatibility.

What to look for in a Canadian provider

  • Trial period — A minimum 30-day trial is standard in Canada; 60–90 days is better. Never buy without one.
  • What's included — Clarify whether follow-up adjustments, cleaning, and maintenance are included in the price or billed separately. Full-service audiology clinics typically bundle these in.
  • Warranty — Look for at least 2–3 years. Costco Canada offers a 3-year warranty; independent audiologists vary.
  • French language service — If your parent's first language is French, confirm the clinic offers service en français. This is particularly important in Quebec and parts of New Brunswick and Ontario.

Province-by-Province Subsidy Guide

Canadian hearing aid subsidies vary considerably by province. The figures below reflect programs as of mid-2026 — eligibility requirements and funding amounts change, so always verify current details directly with the program before relying on them.

Ontario — Assistive Devices Program (ADP)

Ontario's ADP is one of the most accessible provincial programs. It provides up to $500 per ear (i.e., $1,000 per pair) for eligible adults 19 and older, claimable every five years. Children and youth under 19 can access funding every three years. You need a written referral from your audiologist and approval before purchasing. ADP funding is accepted at both private clinics and Costco Canada Hearing Centres. OHIP itself does not cover hearing aids, but ADP meaningfully reduces costs.

Contact: ServiceOntario or ask your audiologist for an ADP referral form.

British Columbia — Medical Services Plan & Community Living BC

BC offers full funding for hearing aids for low-income individuals holding a valid Medical Services Plan (MSP) who meet the financial assessment criteria. The assessment considers income, expenses, and savings. As of June 2026, BC also leads the country in OTC hearing aid access — adults 18+ with mild to moderate loss can now buy OTC aids without a prescription at select retailers.

Contact: Your BC audiologist or MSP client relations for current eligibility criteria.

Alberta — Aids to Daily Living (AADL)

Alberta's AADL program helps residents with the cost of hearing aids through a cost-share model. Eligible Albertans pay a portion (based on income) and the province covers the rest. The program covers a defined list of approved hearing aid models — your audiologist can tell you which ones qualify. Seniors receiving AISH (Assured Income for the Severely Handicapped) or GIS may qualify for additional support.

Contact: Alberta Health Services or your audiologist for AADL authorization forms.

Manitoba — Senior Hearing Aid Grant

Manitoba introduced a grant program specifically for seniors on fixed incomes, providing up to 80% reimbursement of hearing aid costs. This is one of the most generous provincial programs in Canada and was designed specifically to address the cost barrier for seniors. Eligibility is income-tested.

Contact: Manitoba Health or a Manitoba audiologist for current application details.

Quebec — RAMQ and Regional Programs

Quebec's Régie de l'assurance maladie du Québec (RAMQ) covers audiology assessments when referred by a physician. For hearing aid devices, contact your local CLSC (Centre local de services communautaires) — regional health authorities (CISSS/CIUSSS) have their own programs for low-income seniors that aren't always widely advertised. Seniors enrolled in the Régime québécois d'assurance parentale or receiving low-income support may have additional options.

Contact: Your local CLSC — this is the single best starting point for Quebec seniors.

Other Provinces & Territories

  • Saskatchewan — Saskatchewan Aids to Independent Living (SAIL) covers some hearing aids for eligible residents. Contact your regional health authority.
  • Nova Scotia — The Hearing Aid Program provides support to low-income Nova Scotians. Contact the Department of Seniors and Long-term Care.
  • New Brunswick, PEI, Newfoundland — Each province has programs for children and low-income adults. Contact your provincial Department of Health or a local audiologist for current details.
  • Yukon, NWT, Nunavut — Extended Health Benefits through territorial health programs may cover hearing aids. Contact your territorial health authority.

Federal Programs

  • Veterans Affairs Canada — Veterans may receive full hearing aid coverage through the VAC hearing loss program. This can be substantial — contact VAC directly.
  • Non-Insured Health Benefits (NIHB) — First Nations and Inuit individuals are covered for hearing aids under federal NIHB. Contact Health Canada's NIHB program.
  • Disability Tax Credit (DTC) — Qualifying Canadians with significant hearing impairment may be eligible for the DTC, which provides ongoing federal tax relief and access to the Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP). Discuss with your audiologist and a tax advisor.
  • Medical Expense Tax Credit (METC) — Hearing aid purchases are eligible for the METC on your federal tax return, regardless of province. Keep all receipts.

Our Recommendation for Canadian Families

The right hearing aid for your parent depends on four things: the degree of their hearing loss, your budget, your location (city vs. rural), and whether they're comfortable with technology.

For most Canadian seniors, we'd suggest this path: Start with a referral to an audiologist from the family doctor (often covered provincially). Get the audiogram. If you're in a city with Costco access and budget is the primary concern, Costco Canada Hearing is an excellent starting point — comparable technology at roughly half the price of private clinics. If the best possible speech understanding in difficult environments is the priority and budget allows, Phonak Audéo Lumity or Oticon Intent are the top-tier choices. For mild hearing loss, especially in BC where OTC aids are now available, or for seniors in rural areas, the Jabra Enhance Select 300 with remote audiologist support is worth considering.

Whatever you choose: take advantage of the trial period, apply for your provincial subsidy before purchasing (not after), and don't rush the fitting process — proper programming makes as much difference as the device itself.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are hearing aids covered by provincial health plans in Canada?

Coverage varies significantly by province. Ontario's ADP covers up to $500 per ear every five years for adults. Manitoba provides up to 80% reimbursement for low-income seniors. Alberta's AADL program uses a cost-share model. BC covers full costs for low-income individuals on the Medical Services Plan. Federal programs cover Veterans and First Nations/Inuit. Always verify current eligibility directly with your provincial program, as these change regularly.

Can I buy over-the-counter hearing aids in Canada?

As of mid-2026, British Columbia became the first province to allow adults 18+ with mild to moderate hearing loss to purchase Health Canada-approved OTC hearing aids without a prescription. Ontario launched a consultation in April 2026 and is expected to follow. In most other provinces, hearing aids still require dispensing through a licensed audiologist or hearing instrument specialist.

What is the average cost of hearing aids in Canada?

Costco Canada Hearing Centres average around $1,674 CAD per pair. Mid-range prescription aids typically run $2,500–$4,500 CAD per pair. Premium brands like Phonak and Oticon can be $4,000–$6,000+ CAD per pair. Most prices at full-service clinics include fitting and follow-up adjustments.

Where is the best place to buy hearing aids in Canada?

Costco Canada Hearing Centres offer the best value (~$1,674/pair, free fitting included). Independent audiologists provide the most personalized care and widest brand selection. National chains like HearingLife Canada and HearCare Canada are widely available. In BC, select retail locations now carry OTC hearing aids. Confirm what's included in the price (fitting, follow-ups, warranty) before purchasing anywhere.

Does OHIP cover hearing aids in Ontario?

OHIP does not cover hearing aids. However, Ontario's Assistive Devices Program (ADP) provides a grant of up to $500 per ear for eligible adults, claimable every five years. You need an audiologist referral and ADP approval before purchasing. Private employer insurance often provides additional coverage on top of ADP.

What is the difference between prescription and OTC hearing aids?

Prescription hearing aids are fitted and programmed by a licensed audiologist based on your specific audiogram. They work for all degrees of hearing loss. OTC hearing aids (available in BC as of 2026) are self-fitted using an app and are designed for mild to moderate hearing loss. For seniors with significant hearing loss or complex needs, prescription aids typically deliver better outcomes. Most provincial subsidy programs apply to prescription aids, not OTC devices.
