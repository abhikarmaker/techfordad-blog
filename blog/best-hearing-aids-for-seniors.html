My dad resisted hearing aids for years. When OTC options became legal in 2022 and prices dropped, he finally tried one. It changed how we eat dinner together. This guide covers what's now available without a prescription.

About 1 in 3 adults over 65 has hearing loss — making it the third most common chronic condition affecting older Americans. Yet the average person waits 7 years from when they first notice hearing difficulty to when they seek treatment. The primary reason: cost and access barriers to traditional prescription hearing aids, which average $4,600 per pair.

That changed in October 2022, when the FDA created an OTC hearing aid category, allowing adults with mild-to-moderate hearing loss to buy hearing aids directly — no audiologist required. Prices now start under $300. We researched 7 of the most widely-reviewed OTC hearing aids to find the best options for seniors.

Signs of mild-to-moderate hearing loss (OTC appropriate): difficulty following conversation in noisy restaurants; asking people to repeat themselves; turning up TV volume; missing some words in conversation.

Signs that prescription hearing aids are needed: difficulty hearing even in quiet rooms; missing much of what people say; can't hear phone conversations clearly; hearing loss interfering with daily safety.

Will Medicare Pay for a Hearing Aid?

Standard Medicare (Parts A and B): No. Hearing aids, fittings, and hearing exams for the purpose of prescribing hearing aids are not covered.

Medicare Advantage plans: Often. Many Medicare Advantage (Part C) plans offer a hearing benefit — commonly $500 to $2,500 per year toward hearing aids. Plans also often include annual hearing exams. Call your parent's plan to ask about their specific hearing benefit.

FSA/HSA eligible: Yes. OTC hearing aids are FSA and HSA eligible, allowing tax-free purchase.

Medicaid: Varies by state. Some state Medicaid programs cover hearing aids. Contact your state Medicaid office or Area Agency on Aging.

Questions People Ask Before Buying

What is the best hearing aid for seniors in 2026?
For mild-to-moderate hearing loss: Sony CRE-10 (~$999/pair) offers self-fitting via app and near-prescription sound quality without an audiologist visit. Best value: Jabra Enhance Select 300 (~$1,695/pair) with outstanding 30-hour battery. Budget pick: Audien Atom Pro (~$299/pair) with no app needed. For moderate-to-severe loss or complex hearing profiles: see an audiologist for prescription aids.
What is the difference between OTC and prescription hearing aids?
OTC hearing aids (FDA-approved since 2022) self-fit via smartphone app and cost $300–$3,000/pair — appropriate for mild-to-moderate hearing loss, no audiologist needed. Prescription hearing aids require an audiologist and audiogram, cost $3,000–$8,000/pair, and handle any level of hearing loss. If unsure which your parent needs, an audiogram (often free or low-cost at Costco Hearing Center) is the right first step.
Does Medicare cover hearing aids?
Standard Medicare does not. Many Medicare Advantage plans include a hearing benefit of $500–$2,500/year — call your parent's plan to check. OTC hearing aids are FSA and HSA eligible for tax-free purchase. Some state Medicaid programs also cover hearing aids — contact your state Medicaid office to ask.
Are cheap hearing aids worth buying?
Under $100 "hearing amplifiers" (PSAPs) are not hearing aids — they amplify everything equally and can worsen speech clarity. Legitimate OTC hearing aids start around $299 (Audien Atom Pro) and include frequency-specific amplification for speech clarity. For anyone with real hearing difficulty, spending $800–$1,700 on a quality OTC aid like Jabra Enhance Select produces dramatically better results than cheap amplifiers.
How long do hearing aids last?
Most hearing aids last 3–7 years. Behind-the-ear styles tend to last longer than in-ear styles because electronics sit outside the ear canal (less moisture damage). OTC hearing aids with rechargeable batteries typically hold battery capacity for several years before needing replacement. Store in their charging case when not in use to maximize longevity.
