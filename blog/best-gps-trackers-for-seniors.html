When my dad started getting confused on walks near his own house, GPS tracking went from 'maybe someday' to a same-week purchase. I bought three wrong devices before landing on what actually worked — this guide skips the wrong ones.

If your parent has dementia, a tendency to wander, or simply goes out alone and you worry — a GPS tracker can give your whole family peace of mind without taking away their independence. But not all trackers work the same way, and picking the wrong type can leave you with a false sense of security.

We researched 8 GPS and location-tracking devices, evaluating real-time accuracy, battery life, subscription costs, ease of use, and what matters most in wandering scenarios based on caregiver reviews and published specifications. Here's what actually stands out.

🔬 How We Ranked These GPS Trackers

Our rankings are based on manufacturer-published specifications, independent GPS accuracy data, and analysis of verified customer reviews — with particular focus on experiences from dementia caregivers and families managing wandering risk across different coverage environments.

A key differentiator in our research: update frequency. AngelSense's 10-second location updates stand in sharp contrast to trackers that update every 1–5 minutes — a meaningful gap when a fast-walking senior can cover significant distance in that window. This distinction shapes every ranking on this page.

⚠️ GPS vs. Bluetooth — Know the Difference Before You Buy

A real GPS tracker uses cellular networks and works anywhere. A Bluetooth tracker (like Apple AirTag) only updates when a nearby phone detects it. For wandering seniors, you need real GPS. We test and note which is which for every device below.

What to Actually Look For Before You Buy

1. Real GPS vs. Bluetooth Trackers

This is the most important distinction. Real GPS trackers (AngelSense, Jiobit, Tracki, LandAirSea) use cellular networks to continuously report location — they work anywhere there's cell coverage. Bluetooth trackers like the Apple AirTag only update when a nearby phone detects them and relays the signal anonymously through the Find My network. In a busy area this works reasonably well; in a quiet neighborhood it can mean waiting hours for a location update. For seniors who may wander, always choose a real cellular GPS device.

2. Form Factor: Wearable vs. Pocket vs. Vehicle

The best tracker is one your parent will actually have with them. A beautiful wearable GPS is useless if they refuse to wear it. Consider your parent's habits: if they always carry a bag, a small tracker like Tracki slips in unobtrusively. If they drive regularly, a magnetic vehicle tracker like the LandAirSea 54 attaches in 30 seconds. If wandering is the concern, a clothing-attached device like AngelSense — which uses a special loop and pouch that's harder to remove — may be necessary.

3. Update Frequency Matters

Trackers advertise different update rates: every 10 seconds (AngelSense), every 30 seconds, every minute, or only when triggered by movement. For dementia wandering, you want the fastest update rate possible. A senior can walk three blocks in 3 minutes. If your tracker only updates every 5 minutes, they've covered significant distance before you even know they've moved.

4. Geofencing Is a Must-Have

Geofencing lets you draw a virtual boundary around a location — home, a day program, a family member's house. When your parent crosses that boundary, you get an instant alert. This is far more useful than checking an app manually. Every tracker on our list offers geofencing except the Apple AirTag. If you're considering a budget tracker not on our list, make sure it includes this feature.

5. GPS Trackers Don't Replace Medical Alert Systems

A GPS tracker tells you where your parent is. A medical alert system calls for help when they need it. They solve different problems. Many caregivers eventually use both. If you haven't already, see our Best Medical Alert Systems of 2026 review.

6. How to Get a Resistant Senior to Accept a GPS Tracker

Many adult children buy an excellent GPS tracker, then discover their parent refuses to wear it. This is one of the most common challenges in senior care technology. Here's what actually works:

  • Frame it as peace of mind for you, not surveillance of them: "Mum, this would help me worry less when you go out" lands better than "I want to track you." Make it about your anxiety, not their competence.
  • Choose the least visible form factor: A Jiobit clip on a keychain looks like a regular fob. The AirTag in a wallet case is invisible. Avoid devices that look medical or clinical for parents who are sensitive about this.
  • For dementia patients, start early: A parent with mild cognitive impairment can agree to wear a tracker. A parent in moderate dementia may remove it repeatedly. Starting the conversation and habit early — while they still have capacity to understand — is much easier than introducing it later.
  • Involve their doctor: A recommendation from the GP often carries more authority than one from an adult child. Ask the doctor to mention fall detection or location tracking at the next appointment.
  • In severe cases, use clothing integration: AngelSense's velcro pouch attaches to a bra strap or waistband and is specifically designed to be difficult to remove without assistance. Some families use a shoe pouch with a Tracki for parents who repeatedly remove wrist or body-worn devices.

7. GPS Tracker Battery Life — What to Expect

Battery life is one of the most misunderstood specs in GPS trackers. Here's what the numbers actually mean:

  • AngelSense: 24 hours. Needs daily charging, which becomes part of the care routine. Most families charge it overnight. Not ideal if your parent refuses to take it off or hand it over for charging.
  • Jiobit: 1–2 days. Excellent for a small device. The charging dock is compact and magnetic — easy for the caregiver to place it each night without the senior needing to handle cables.
  • Tracki standard: 3–5 days. With the extended battery case, 30+ days. This makes it suitable for tucking into a bag or clothing pocket that isn't checked daily.
  • Apple AirTag: ~1 year. The year-long battery is one of the AirTag's biggest advantages — truly set and forget. Battery is user-replaceable (standard CR2032 coin cell).
  • LandAirSea 54: 2–4 weeks magnetic, indefinite hardwired. If hardwired to the vehicle's battery, it never needs charging at all. This is the recommended installation for long-term vehicle monitoring.

8. Red Flags — GPS Trackers to Avoid

With dozens of GPS trackers on Amazon, many are poor quality. Watch for these warning signs:

  • No monthly fee trackers claiming "real GPS": Real cellular GPS tracking requires cellular data, which costs money. A tracker claiming "no subscription, works anywhere" is either Bluetooth-only or has severe limitations not disclosed in the listing.
  • 2G-only trackers: Most carriers shut down 2G networks in 2022. A tracker listing "GSM/2G" will have poor or no coverage. Look for 4G LTE.
  • Update intervals listed as "1 minute" or slower: For senior tracking, 1-minute updates are acceptable for low-risk situations. For dementia wandering or active monitoring, you need trackers that update every 10–30 seconds.
  • No geofencing: Geofencing alerts are the most important active safety feature. If a tracker doesn't offer geofencing, it requires you to check the app manually — which means you might check hours after your parent wandered.
  • Apps with poor reviews: The hardware matters less than the software. A GPS tracker with an unreliable or confusing app is worse than useless in an emergency. Check Google Play and App Store reviews specifically for reliability and notification performance.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a GPS tracker and a Bluetooth tracker like AirTag?
A real GPS tracker uses cellular networks to report location in real time from anywhere — no matter where your parent is. A Bluetooth tracker like the Apple AirTag only updates location when a nearby Apple device detects it and pings the Find My network. For a senior who may wander into unfamiliar areas, a dedicated cellular GPS tracker is far more reliable.
Will my parent know they're being tracked?
This depends on the device. Wearable trackers like AngelSense and Jiobit are visible on the body. The LandAirSea 54 is typically hidden in a vehicle. For seniors with dementia, families often attach a tracker to a shoe or clothing pouch. It is generally considered best practice to inform the senior about tracking when they have the capacity to understand — this preserves dignity and trust.
Can a GPS tracker replace a medical alert system?
No. A GPS tracker shows you where your parent is, but it cannot call for emergency help on their behalf. A medical alert system with a monitoring center can dispatch paramedics even if your parent is unconscious. For the highest level of safety, many families use both: a medical alert system for emergency response plus a GPS tracker for location awareness.
How much does GPS tracking cost per month?
Budget options like Tracki start at around $19.99/month for unlimited tracking. Mid-range devices like Jiobit run $8.99–$14.99/month. AngelSense, designed specifically for memory care, starts at $39.99/month. Apple AirTag 2 has no monthly fee but requires an iPhone and uses crowd-sourced Bluetooth rather than true GPS.
What is the best GPS tracker for a parent with Alzheimer's or dementia?
AngelSense is the clear recommendation for dementia and Alzheimer's patients. It's the only tracker specifically designed for memory care, with 10-second location updates, geofencing alerts that call your whole family list simultaneously, a listen-in audio feature, and a velcro attachment system that is difficult for a confused senior to remove. For severe dementia cases, families often integrate it into a shoe or clothing pouch rather than wearing it on the wrist where it can be removed.
Can you track a senior without them knowing?
Technically, yes — the LandAirSea 54 in a vehicle and a Tracki in a bag can operate without the senior's knowledge. However, experts in elder care ethics generally recommend informing your parent that a tracker is in use, both out of respect for their dignity and because covert tracking can damage trust if discovered. For parents with severe dementia who lack the capacity to understand, families often make a practical decision in consultation with the senior's doctor. If you're tracking someone in a vehicle you own, this is generally legal in most jurisdictions — but check your local privacy laws.
Do GPS trackers work indoors?
Standard GPS signals have difficulty penetrating buildings — GPS relies on line-of-sight to satellites. However, the best trackers on our list use multiple positioning methods: Jiobit uses LTE + WiFi + Bluetooth together, which provides much better indoor accuracy than GPS alone. AngelSense also uses cellular-based positioning indoors. A device relying solely on GPS will lose accuracy or signal when your parent is inside a building, mall, or hospital — this is a key reason to look for multi-network trackers.
What is a GPS watch for elderly parents and which is the best?
A GPS watch for seniors combines a wristwatch form factor with real-time cellular location tracking. The best wearable GPS option in 2026 is the Jiobit Smart Tag — it can be worn on a wristband or attached to clothing and provides 10-second location updates with geofencing alerts. For memory care specifically, AngelSense on a velcro wrist attachment is the most reliable because its velcro system makes it harder to remove than a standard watch clasp. The main limitation of wrist-worn GPS devices: seniors with dementia often remove wristwatches, which is why many families use the AngelSense sewn into a shoe or clothing instead.
Are there GPS trackers with no monthly fee?
Real-time cellular GPS trackers require a data subscription to transmit location — there is no way around this. The only meaningful "no monthly fee" GPS-adjacent option is the Apple AirTag 2 ($29 one-time), which uses Bluetooth crowd-sourcing through Apple's Find My network rather than true GPS. This works well in cities but can be unreliable in less-trafficked areas. Among cellular trackers, Tracki has the lowest plan at $19.99/month. If you see a GPS tracker advertised as "no monthly fee" with real-time tracking, read the fine print — they almost always require a SIM card plan purchased separately, or they use Bluetooth only with serious limitations.
