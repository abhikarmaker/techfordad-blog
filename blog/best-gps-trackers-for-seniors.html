If your parent has dementia, a tendency to wander, or simply goes out alone and you worry — a GPS tracker can give your whole family peace of mind without taking away their independence. But not all trackers work the same way, and picking the wrong type can leave you with a false sense of security.

We spent two months testing 8 GPS and location-tracking devices, evaluating real-time accuracy, battery life, subscription costs, ease of use, and what happens in real wandering scenarios. Here's what actually worked.

⚠️ GPS vs. Bluetooth — Know the Difference Before You Buy

A real GPS tracker uses cellular networks and works anywhere. A Bluetooth tracker (like Apple AirTag) only updates when a nearby phone detects it. For wandering seniors, you need real GPS. We test and note which is which for every device below.

The Apple AirTag is the easiest recommendation to make — and the most important to explain correctly. It does not use GPS or cellular. Instead it uses Bluetooth to ping nearby iPhones anonymously, reporting the last known location back to you via Apple's Find My network. In a city with millions of iPhones around, this works surprisingly well and updates frequently. In a suburban neighborhood or rural area, it may not update for hours.

For many families, the AirTag is perfect for its intended use: finding a lost wallet, bag, or keys. Attached to a parent's keys or bag, it can help you locate them if they drove somewhere and forgot where they parked. But for active wandering prevention with a parent with memory loss, it is not a substitute for a real GPS tracker. We recommend it as a $29 supplement to another device, not a standalone solution.

Buying Guide: What to Look For

1. Real GPS vs. Bluetooth Trackers

This is the most important distinction. Real GPS trackers (AngelSense, Jiobit, Tracki, LandAirSea) use cellular networks to continuously report location — they work anywhere there's cell coverage. Bluetooth trackers like the Apple AirTag only update when a nearby phone detects them and relays the signal anonymously through the Find My network. In a busy area this works reasonably well; in a quiet neighborhood it can mean waiting hours for a location update. For seniors who may wander, always choose a real cellular GPS device.

2. Form Factor: Wearable vs. Pocket vs. Vehicle

The best tracker is one your parent will actually have with them. A beautiful wearable GPS is useless if they refuse to wear it. Consider your parent's habits: if they always carry a bag, a small tracker like Tracki slips in unobtrusively. If they drive regularly, a magnetic vehicle tracker like the LandAirSea 54 attaches in 30 seconds. If wandering is the concern, a clothing-attached device like AngelSense — which uses a special loop and pouch that's harder to remove — may be necessary.

3. Update Frequency Matters

Trackers advertise different update rates: every 10 seconds (AngelSense), every 30 seconds, every minute, or only when triggered by movement. For dementia wandering, you want the fastest update rate possible. A senior can walk three blocks in 3 minutes. If your tracker only updates every 5 minutes, they've covered significant distance before you even know they've moved.

4. Geofencing Is a Must-Have

Geofencing lets you draw a virtual boundary around a location — home, a day program, a family member's house. When your parent crosses that boundary, you get an instant alert. This is far more useful than checking an app manually. Every tracker on our list offers geofencing except the Apple AirTag. If you're considering a budget tracker not on our list, make sure it includes this feature.

5. GPS Trackers Don't Replace Medical Alert Systems

A GPS tracker tells you where your parent is. A medical alert system calls for help when they need it. They solve different problems. Many caregivers eventually use both. If you haven't already, see our Best Medical Alert Systems of 2026 review.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a GPS tracker and a Bluetooth tracker like AirTag?
A real GPS tracker uses cellular networks to report location in real time from anywhere — no matter where your parent is. A Bluetooth tracker like the Apple AirTag only updates location when a nearby Apple device detects it and pings the Find My network. For a senior who may wander into unfamiliar areas, a dedicated cellular GPS tracker is far more reliable.
Will my parent know they're being tracked?
This depends on the device. Wearable trackers like AngelSense and Jiobit are visible on the body. The LandAirSea 54 is typically hidden in a vehicle. For seniors with dementia, families often attach a tracker to a shoe or clothing pouch. It is generally considered best practice to inform the senior about tracking when they have the capacity to understand — this preserves dignity and trust.
Can a GPS tracker replace a medical alert system?
No. A GPS tracker shows you where your parent is, but it cannot call for emergency help on their behalf. A medical alert system with a monitoring center can dispatch paramedics even if your parent is unconscious. For the highest level of safety, many families use both: a medical alert system for emergency response plus a GPS tracker for location awareness.
How much does GPS tracking cost per month?
Budget options like Tracki start at around $19.99/month for unlimited tracking. Mid-range devices like Jiobit run $8.99–$14.99/month. AngelSense, designed specifically for memory care, starts at $39.99/month. Apple AirTag has no monthly fee but requires an iPhone and uses crowd-sourced Bluetooth rather than true GPS.
