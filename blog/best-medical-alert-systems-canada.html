Choosing a medical alert system in Canada is different from the US market. Services like Life Alert don't operate here, and Canada has its own well-established options — including the non-profit MedicAlert Foundation and Telus LivingWell, backed by one of Canada's largest telecoms.

This guide covers the main medical alert services available to Canadians, with Canadian pricing in CAD and notes on what to look for in each province. Always confirm current plans and pricing directly with the provider, as these can change.

Canada Medical Alert Buying Guide

Key differences from US systems

  • Life Alert does not operate in Canada — if you've seen it advertised, those are US-only plans
  • Cellular network matters — for GPS mobile devices, ask which Canadian carrier the device uses (Telus, Rogers, or Bell). This affects rural coverage significantly
  • Landline requirement — older home base stations often require a landline; cellular-only options are better for households that have cut the cord
  • Bilingual support — if your parent's first language is French, confirm the monitoring centre offers service en français before signing up
  • Contract terms — always ask about minimum contract length and early cancellation fees before committing

Questions to ask before signing up

  • Where is the monitoring centre located, and is it staffed by Canadians?
  • Which cellular network does the mobile unit run on?
  • Is there a minimum contract, and what are the cancellation terms?
  • Does the device work in my parent's specific area (including rural coverage)?
  • Is fall detection automatic, or does the senior need to press a button?
  • What happens if the power goes out — does the base station have a backup battery?

Financial Assistance Programs

Unlike the US where Medicare generally does not cover medical alert devices, some Canadian provinces and territories have programs that may help offset costs. Availability and eligibility vary widely by province and individual circumstances. These programs change — always verify current information with your provincial government or a local seniors' resource centre.

  • Ontario — Ontario's Assistive Devices Program (ADP) funds certain assistive technologies. Home care coordinators through Ontario Health at Home (formerly LHIN) may also be able to connect you with subsidized options. Contact Ontario Health at Home in your region to ask.
  • British Columbia — BC's Home and Community Care program supports seniors living independently. A home care coordinator can advise whether any funding is available for personal emergency response systems in your specific situation.
  • Alberta — Alberta Continuing Care programs support eligible seniors. Contact Alberta Health Services for guidance on available assistance in your area.
  • Quebec — Some CISSS and CIUSSS regional health authorities offer support programs for seniors. Contact your local CLSC (Centre local de services communautaires) for current information.
  • Other provinces — Most provinces have seniors' services programs. Contact your provincial Ministry of Health or a local seniors' centre for the most up-to-date information on what's available in your region.

Tip: Before paying out of pocket, ask your family doctor or a home care coordinator — they often know about funding options that aren't widely advertised.

Choosing the Right System for Your Family

For most Canadian families, the starting point comes down to two questions: Does your parent need GPS and outdoor coverage, or primarily home coverage? And how important is professional monitoring vs. directly calling 911?

MedicAlert Canada is a strong choice if your parent has a complex medical history and you want first responders to have immediate access to that information. Telus LivingWell is worth considering if rural coverage is a priority. Lifeline Canada is well recognized and has a long track record. The Apple Watch SE works well for active, iPhone-owning seniors who want a safety net without a monthly subscription.

Always check current pricing directly with each provider — plans and pricing change, and the figures in any article (including this one) may be out of date.
